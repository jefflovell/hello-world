Building a Neural Network

Start with human-readable facts about the world, translate them into machine-readable numbers, and assemble one complete prediction from the inside out.

To the uninitiated, a neural network diagram can look like an arcane pictogram: circles, arrows, weights, hidden layers, and algebraic labels all pretending to be obvious. In reality, the diagram is a visual language for tracking how numbers move through a model. Computer science does this constantly. We talk about queues and stacks, heaps and trees, addresses and memory, parents and children. Neural networks belong to that same tradition: useful symbols for reasoning about an abstract machine.

This is not how biological neurons work, and it is not how humans think. It is a structured analogy. The danger is that the analogy often hides the actual arithmetic behind a few circles and lines. In this lesson we will peel back that symbolism one operation at a time.

Throughout this multi-part lab we will grow a classic neural network: the multi-layer perceptron. MLP-style feedforward layers are one of the core building blocks inside modern systems like Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Grok. We will start with real-world features that describe an object or idea, turn those features into a vector, pass that vector through neurons, and assemble a feedforward network that can make a useful prediction.

What are we actually modeling?

One title. One audience. One prediction.

This is a stripped-down regression model. We give the network one library title and one audience segment, then ask it for a number: what percentage of exposed viewers are likely to complete the title during its first week? Not “is this movie good?” Not “will everyone love it?” Just one narrower question: if this title appears in front of this audience, how much attention might it earn?

That narrowness is the point. A real recommender system is a city of machinery: retrieval systems gather candidates, ranking systems compare them, business rules reshape the list, and feedback loops keep changing the ground underneath. We are not building that city yet. We are taking one small room, turning on the lights, and inspecting the math inside.

One system. Three different descriptions.

Object. Arithmetic. Algebra.

We will build one network three ways at once: first as a diagram, then as arithmetic, then as the compact algebra engineers use when the system gets large.

Object

Nodes and edges

The diagram is a kind of graph. The circles are nodes, or neurons, and the lines are edges. Nodes hold numbers; edges show which numbers can influence each other.

Arithmetic

One operation at a time

In a neural net, edges carry weights: numbers that strengthen, weaken, or reverse an input. A neuron multiplies, adds, then passes the result through an activation function.

Algebra

Many operations together

Linear algebra lets us write many tiny calculations at once. Feature vectors, weight matrices, and activation vectors turn a pile of arithmetic into one feedforward prediction.

The behavior layer, compressed

These numbers are not magic.

Before a neural network ever sees a vector, another system has already turned messy human behavior into measured signals. For this lesson, we compress that upstream machinery into three inspectable teaching features.

01 · Audience–title affinity

Does this audience lean toward this title?

Could come from
Past watches, searches, clicks, completions, skips, likes, and similarity to neighboring titles.
Why useful
Behavior often reveals preference before a person can name it. The trail of choices is noisy, but it is rarely empty.
Why imperfect
New users and titles have little history, and yesterday’s recommendations can shape today’s “preference.”
02 · Title awareness

Has this title entered the room?

Could come from
Campaign reach, trailer views, impressions, searches, franchise familiarity, and talent recognition.
Why useful
People rarely choose a title they never notice. Awareness can move first-week behavior before taste has a chance to speak.
Why imperfect
Awareness is not affection, and recommendations can create the very awareness they later try to measure.
03 · Tone preference match

Does the title feel like the mood this audience seeks?

Could come from
Content metadata or embeddings matched against prior behavior: lighthearted, serious, tense, comforting, fast, or slow.
Why useful
Genre is a blunt instrument. Two horror films, comedies, or dramas can ask for completely different kinds of attention.
Why imperfect
Tone is subjective, context changes taste, and one cohort score hides the unruly differences among individuals.
What was abstracted away?

Individual user sequences, item embeddings, device and session context, availability, novelty, household composition, candidate retrieval, exploration, business rules, and feedback loops. The lesson keeps the vector small so the mechanism can be seen.
